PRE_Sections

​Considerations prior to updating of establishment details (added 3 June 2021)

The following should be considered prior to updating establishment details:

  • When an establishment notifies the Department of an administrative change (that is, a change of occupier, company name, address, or in some instances product specific changes), the Department will amend the establishment record in the system.
  • The establishment's amended details will appear on applicable export documentation including health certification from the date that the Department amends the establishment record
  • When relevant changes are made to an establishment's details, the Department will advise trading partners that maintain establishment lists of the change of details; however, the Department is unable to guarantee a timeframe as to when trading partners will update the establishment's overseas listing details they maintain. This means that there may be a period in which export certification issued by the Department does not match the trading partner's official published listing, which may result in clearance issues at the port of entry.
  • Establishments should be aware that their ability to export to some countries may be impacted during this transition period and may include issues with goods in transit.
  • Exporters and manufacturers are encouraged to check that their establishment's details are recorded accurately where they appear on trading partners' lists and to contact the Department if errors are found.

1.4 General information

PMA_GeneralInformation

Exporters are likely to be asked by importers to demonstrate conformity of exporter milk and dairy products with the CU Milk Regulation (Decision 67) (PDF 150 KB) and its Annex (PDF 2.0 MB).

Conformity of imported milk and dairy products is assessed either by the product going through a state registration approval process or through declarations of conformity (Language: Russian – PDF 230 KB) (see additional link for a translated version of a declaration form (Language: English – PDF 250 KB)).

Products that require state registration

State registration is required for the following milk and dairy products:

  • those intended for consumption by infants, children, pregnant women and nursing women
  • those intended for use as therapeutic and prophylactic dietary products
  • those intended for use as nutrition products for athletes
  • biologically active dietary supplements
  • raw materials for production of biologically active dietary supplements
  • organic milk and dairy products and
  • those originating from genetically engineered or modified (transgenic) animals

Details of the process of applying for state registration for these specific dairy products are outlined in the CU Food Safety Regulation (Decision 880 dated 9 December 2011) (PDF 680 KB) and the Single List of of goods subject to sanitary-and-epidemiologic supervision (control) at the border and territory of the CU (Decision 299) (PDF 180 KB).

Declarations of conformity

Declarations of conformity must be provided by importers for milk and dairy products that have not been state registered.

Declarations of conformity (Language: Russian – PDF 230 KB) contain the following information:

  • name and location of the applicant (importer)
  • name and location of the manufacturer
  • information about the object of confirmation of conformity allowing identification of the product
  • name of the technical regulations of the CU that apply to the product (that is, for dairy products – CU Milk Regulation (Decision 67) (PDF 150 KB)
  • application of the applicant concerning safety of the product if used in accordance with the intended purpose and adoption of measures to ensure conformance of the product to the requirements of CU technical regulations (that is, for dairy products – CU Milk Regulation (Decision 67) (PDF 150 KB)
  • information about tests and other documents that demonstrate conformity of the product with CU requirements (CU Milk Regulation (Decision 67) (PDF 150 KB) and
  • the duration of the declaration. (According to the CU Milk Regulation (Decision 67) (PDF 150 KB), the duration of conformity declarations for commercially produced dairy products will be 3 years).

After making a declaration of conformity which is based on documentary review and testing, applicants are required to mark products with a unified mark so products can be circulated on to markets in CU countries.

More information about declarations of conformity and marking are outlined in the CU Food Safety Regulation (Decision 880 dated 9 December 2011) (PDF 670 KB) and the CU Milk Regulation (Decision 67) (Language: English – PDF 150 KB). Also refer to the translated version of a declaration form (Language: Russian to English – PDF 250 KB).

Note
The Eurasian Economic Committee has issued a notification which outlines that there may be a transitional period involving some elements of the implementation of the marking requirements.

2.1 Milk and milk products

PPHC_MilkMilkProducts

Milk and milk products of bovine origin are defined as prescribed goods under the Export Control Act. Production of these products for export is regulated under the Export Control (Milk and Milk Products) Rules 2021.

Milk and milk products manufactured from non-bovine species, for example buffalo, goat and sheep, may also need to be regulated through the Export Control (Milk and Milk Products) Rules 2021, though this is only required when export registration and health certification from the department is required by the Russian Federation.  

We are not aware that the Russian Federation has any known additional requirements for the production and export of non-bovine milk and milk products.

Exporters should note that products will need to be labelled with the uniform mark of the Customs Union (CU) on the basis of declarations of conformity with CU requirements outlined in the Annexes (PDF 2.0 MB) of the CU Milk Regulation (Decision 67 Technical Regulation "On Safety of Milk and Dairy Products (PDF 150 KB). Refer to Sections 1.4. Further information and 7.1. Packaging and labelling.

4.1 Microbial and residue sampling

OR_MicrobialResidueSampling

Microbial sampling of milk and milk products for the Russian Federation can be met through the requirements of the Export Control (Milk and Milk Products) Rules 2021.

Exporters should note that products will need to be labelled with the uniform mark of the Customs Union (CU) on the basis of declarations of conformity with CU requirements outlined in the Annexes (PDF 2.0 MB) of the CU Milk Regulation (Decision 67 Technical Regulation "On Safety of Milk and Dairy Products" (PDF 150 KB). Refer to Sections 1.4. Further information and 7.1. Packaging and labelling.

Russia's requirements were previously listed in Russia's Sanitary Rules and Regulations (SanPin) 2.3.2.1078-01 of 6 November 2001 (PDF 2.5 MB).

Note: Australia has developed a product-specific testing program for tetracycline group antibiotics using Russian Maximum Residue Limits (MRL) of 10µg/kg.

This program augments existing bulk milk testing under the Australian Milk Residue Analysis (AMRA) Survey which tests to the Australian and European Union MRL of 100µg/kg. In order to meet Russia and CU requirements, any detection from this higher sensitivity testing will be notified to the Department, state regulatory authorities and the relevant manufacturer.

The Department will be notified of relevant batch numbers for affected product and will provide final approval on exports in this case, and will ensure any affected products are excluded from entry to the Russian Federation.
Russia residue standards

Maximum residue limits (MRLs) or import tolerances for residues of specific agricultural and veterinary chemicals approved for use in Australia may not exist in Russia or may be established at levels lower than those established in Australia. Maximum Limits (MLs) for contaminants may also vary between Russia and Australia.

Food business operators should note that:

  • These differences can be interrogated case-by-case by considering the Russia ​standards and the Australian standards.
  • Any detection of a residue above the Russia MRLs/MLs (as applicable) at the border may result in rejection of the consignment.
Note
Importing country residue standards can usually be found via the official webpages of their competent authority. Links to a limited number of export markets and Australia are provided on the DAFF website.

4.3 Hygiene

OR_Hygiene

Hygiene requirements for the Russian Federation can be met through the requirements in the Export Control (Milk and Milk Products) Rules 2021.

Russian requirements for dairy production are outlined in Customs Union (CU):

  • Milk Regulation (Decision on 67 Technical Regulation "On Safety of Milk and Dairy Products" (PDF 150 KB)
  • Food Safety Regulation (Decision 880 dated 9 December 2011 (PDF 670 KB)

Previous Russian requirements are specified in Federal Law No 88-F3 of 12 June 2008 (PDF 660 KB). In particular, Law No 88 required medical assessments and sanitation training for employees, and washing and disinfection of manufacture areas and equipment.

Although the CU Milk Regulation (PDF 150 KB) does not mention specific requirements for medical certificates, sanitation training and disinfection, there is a requirement in the CU Food Safety Regulation (PDF 670 KB) for medical assessments. Such assessments are required for workers employed in jobs associated with the production (manufacturing) of food products and who come into direct contact with food raw materials and/or food products. These workers must undergo a mandatory preliminary medical examination upon hiring and periodic examinations.

5.1 Milk and milk products

DR_MilkMilkProducts

All consignments require a separate Health Certificate for each container of products exported from Australia to Russia.

Milk and milk products that are prescribed under Australia's export rules

Dairy products prescribed under the Export Control (Milk and Milk Products) Rules 2021 include:

  1. are milk or milk products derived from a bovine animal; and
  2. are intended to be exported as food.
Milk and milk products that are not prescribed under Australia's export rules

Milk and milk products which do not meet the criteria under the Export Control (Milk and Milk Products) Rules 2021 are classified by Australia as non-prescribed goods. It is the responsibility of the exporter to confirm with the importing country if there are any specific requirements for the import of these products. Typically, non-prescribed goods include, but are not limited to, ice-cream, non-bovine milk products, colostrum and products with a minor component of dairy.

Further information on exporting non-prescribed goods from Australia is available on the department’s website.

In some cases, an importing country requires specific health certification and government oversight for what would typically be non-prescribed milk and milk products. When required, these products become prescribed under export legislation and must meet all export and importing country requirements. This includes the products being manufactured and stored at an export registered establishment and must have a valid export permit and health certificate for each consignment. Guidance on export registration can be found at Dairy export registered establishments.

Health certificates for milk and milk products

For milk and milk products produced for human consumption (including milk and milk products classified as a prescribed good by an importing Country), use health certificate template EX325A. The EX325A Certificate contains the following attestations:

Herewith it is certified that:

  1. a)  Dairy products were received at milk processing establishments that hold export licences issued by the central government veterinary service of Australia and are permanently monitored by the government veterinary service. Milk and milk products originate from the premises, localities and healthy cattle which are free from infectious animal diseases, including:
    • rinderpest, foot and mouth disease, infectious pleuropneumonia and vesicular stomatitis – during the last 12 months on the whole territory of the country
    • brucellosis (B. abortus) and tuberculosis – during the last 12 months in the premises
    • the milk/milk products has been provided by animals belonging to herds accredited free of Enzootic Bovine Leucosis
    • brucellosis (B. melitensis) – during the last 2 years in the premises
    • sheep pox and goat pox – during the last 12 months in the premises
  2. b) Anthrax is not prevalent in the district from which the animals were derived
  3. c)  Milk and dairy products exported to the Russian Federation are not infected with Salmonella or other infectious bacterial agents, were not treated with colouring substances, ionising or UV rays
  4. d)  Milk, used in manufacture of milk products, was heat treated sufficiently to remove pathogenic microorganisms dangerous to human health. Milk products were treated to ensure the absence of pathogenic organisms
  5. e)  Milk products are considered fit for human consumption by the competent authority of the exporting country and suitable for unrestricted retail purposes
  6. f)  Animals were not fed with forage of animal origin, which was produced from the internal organs and tissues of ruminants from the spongiform encephalopathy in cattle
  7. g)  Based on the results of an official random testing program, it can be assumed that the milk does not contain residues likely to be harmful to human health of natural and synthetic oestrogenic or hormonal substances, thyrostatics, antibiotics or tranquillisers
  8. h)  Microbiological, chemical, toxicological and radiological quities of the milk and milk products comply with the current Russian veterinary and sanitary rules and requirements
  9. i)  Means of transport for the products are treated and prepared in accordance with the rules approved in Australia
  10. j)  The package and packing material are single use and meet the hygiene requirements

It is a requirement of the Russian Federation that each individual container of dairy product exported to Russia must be accompanied by its own Veterinary Certificate. Exporters must select "separate certificate per container" option when raising their export permit in NEXDOC.

Whenever export documentation is obtained, the exporter must provide a declaration stating that all the information provided in the permit is true and complete. They must also state that they are in possession of a declaration of compliance that:

  1. applies to the milk and milk products
  2. complies with the requirements of the Export Control (Milk and Milk Products) Rules 2021.

The Russian Health Certificate must be signed on or before the date of departure. Replacement Health Certificates cannot be issued after the product has left Australia. The Department may be able to provide a letter addressed to the importing authorities when there is a change of consignee, container number or seal number. This letter may or may not result in consignment clearance.

CU certification requirements

Russian veterinary certificates, such as the EX325A will remain valid until negotiations between Australia and CU countries on the content of CU certificates has been concluded.

Transit certification

Milk and milk products transiting the European Union or the United States may require transit documentation. These certificates are available as additional certificates through the NEXDOC system.

If the product is transiting a European Union member state, see the particular European Union member state regarding the certificate template to select.

If the product is transiting the United States, a ZDUS01 Health Certificate Transit Certificate is required. When product transits a US port and the 'country of final destination' Russia) is not US, US transit certification, including sealing details, is required in addition to the Russian certification.

If the product is transiting a US port and the Department seals are applied, the US should be included as the discharge country or a transit country. Alternatively, a reference should be included in the exporter declaration or inspector comments to indicate that the product may be transiting a US port.

All dairy products to the US should be only of Australian or New Zealand origin and only processed in Australia or New Zealand. Exporters should contact the Department if products contain raw milk that is not produced or not wholly processed in Australia or New Zealand.

PLSM_Labelling

Additional Russian labelling and packaging requirements for milk and milk products are specified in the CU Food Product Labelling Regulation (CU Decision 881 of 9 December 2011) (PDF 175 KB) and CU Milk Regulation (Decision 67 Technical Regulation "On Safety of Milk and Dairy Product" of 9 October 2013) (PDF 150 KB).

Information to be included on labels

Labels on packages of milk processing products shall have marking (labelling) with the following information:

  • name of the product in accordance with the definitions in the CU Milk Regulation (Refer to Section 7.5 Terms used to describe dairy products)
  • mass fraction of fat, fat in solids or milkfat in percent (Note: fat in solids is required for cheese, cheese products, processed cheeses, and processed cheese products)
  • name and location of the manufacturer of products (legal address, including country and address of the production site of products (if different from the legal address)
  • name and location of the organization authorized by the manufacturer to accept claims from consumers in the CU
  • brand mark (trade mark) if the product has a registered brand or trademark
  • net weight or volume (This should placed on consumer package where it can be easily read)
  • information about the batch or lot (for example, the batch number)
  • ingredients including additives listed in decreasing order according to mass fractions. The inscription "Content" shall be put directly before the specification of such ingredients.
  • shelf-life, use-by date or expiry date
  • "manufacture date" or "production date"
  • nutrition indicators, such as energy value (caloric content), quantities of protein, fat, carbohydrates, vitamins and minerals.
  • warning labels or handling marks (e.g. "keep away from sunlight," "temperature limitation," "keep dry," "perishable cargo") are applied selectively, in accordance with the conditions of storage and transportation of milk or dairy products.
  • information on presence of genetically modified organisms (GMO) – only if the product includes GMO ingredients and
  • uniform mark indicating products are compliant and can be circulated within CU member countries (For information about declarations of conformity, refer to section 1.4, the CU Food Safety Regulation (Decision 880 dated 9 December 2011) (PDF 670 KB) and the CU Milk Regulation (Decision 67) (PDF 150 KB))

Labels on concentrated or condensed dairy products or dry (powdered) dairy products shall include the following additional information date of production (manufacturing) and the shelf life of the product (shown on the top or bottom of a can or package). When the shelf life is outlined by the words "best before" or "use before", next to these words the place where such information can be found shall be indicated, for example: "see on the top or bottom of the can in the first or second row" or "see on the top or bottom of the package."

Use of dairy terms
  • Reconstituted milk – The main raw material must be included in the product descriptor – for example, "reconstituted milk from dry milk", "reconstituted milk from concentrated milk", "reconstituted milk from dry and condensed milk"
  • Milk from species other than bovine – The species name should be indicated on the package labels before or after the term "milk" (for example, goat milk).
  • Heat treatment – The method should be indicated on the package labels after the term "milk" or after the names of milk processing products – for example, "milk pasteurized", "cream sterilized"
  • Other processing information – This information may follow name of products – for example, "fermented milk drink," "recombined cream." The term "normalized" in the name of milk and milk processing products may be omitted on the package labels
  • Composite dairy products – Dairy terms shall be supplemented with information on the availability of non-dairy components added to such products – for example, "curd with fruit pieces", "fruit kefir", "processed cheese with ham"
  • The term "product" in the names of dairy product – This word may be replaced or supplemented with a term characterizing the product texture or shape (jelly, cocktail, cream, mousse, paste, roll, sauce, tart, etc), for example: "milk-and-juice cocktail", "sour cream sauce", "curd souffle with nuts", "cheese roll with spices"
  • Fermented milk products (for example, yoghurt, kefir, sour cream, curd) – Labels of a milk-containing or a cultured milk product the words "milk containing" or "cultured" shall be replaced with the terms characterizing production technology of such products – for example, "kefir product", "heat treated kefir product", "yoghurt product", "heat treated yoghurt product"
  • Products with milk fat substitutes – This shall be included in the name on the front of the consumer package – for example, "sour cream product with milk fat substitute"
  • Products made from whole milk - The mass fraction of fat may be given in as a range "from... to..." in percent
  • Flavourings – The name of the ingredient substituted by such flavouring agent and which is not part of the food product, using the words: with the flavour of and (or) with the smell of
  • Food additives – It is obligatory to specify its functional designation (acidity regulator, stabilizator, emulsifier, etc.) and the name of the food additive, which may be substituted for the food additive index in accordance with International Numbering System (INS) or European Numbering System (E). If a food additive has a various functional designation, it shall be specified, correspondingly to its application purpose

  • Sweeteners such as sugar alcohols – Labels shall include the words written directly after the food product content: Contains a sweetener (sweeteners). If overused may provoke laxative action

Ingredients (including food additives, flavouring agents), biologically active additives, the consumption of which may entail allergic reactions or is contraindicated for some kind of diseases and which are listed in below shall be specified in the food product content:

  • peanut and peanut-derived products
  • aspartame and aspartame-acesulfame salt
  • mustard and mustard-derived products
  • sulphur dioxide and sulphites if their total content exceeds 10 milligrams per 1 kilogram or 10 milligrams per one litre in terms of sulphur dioxide
  • cereals, containing gluten and gluten-derived products
  • sesame and sesame-derived products
  • lupin and lupin-derived products
  • molluscs and mollusc-derived products
  • milk and milk-derived products (including lactose)
  • nuts and nut-derived products
  • crustacean and crustacean-derived products
  • fish and fish-derived products (save fish gelatine used as a basis in preparations containing vitamins and carotenoids)
  • celery and celery-derived products
  • soya and soya-derived products
  • eggs and egg-derived products.

Information on physical properties and (or) on special food product treatment (reconstituted, smoked, marinated, ground, ionized, freeze-dried food products and detailed information on it) shall be included in the name of the food product or placed close to the name thereof, in the event the absence of such information may misguide consumers.

It is not allowed to include ingredients in the name of the food product if they or their derived products are not part of the food product.

Definitions established in the CU Milk Regulation for milk, milk products and dairy composite products, including the words or their parts that comprise these definitions, or their different combinations, are not allowed in the names of brand marks (trade marks) when marking milk-containing products (except cultured products), on their labels, for advertising or other purposes that could mislead consumers. For example, the word "butter" or "ice cream" cannot be used in products with milk-fat substitutes.
